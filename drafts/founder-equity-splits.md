# How to Split Founder Equity When "Equal" Isn't Obvious

The equity conversation is the one founders most want to skip. So most skip it, and the skip has a name: 50/50. Sometimes that is the right answer arrived at honestly. Just as often it is a way to avoid an awkward hour, and it is frequently proposed by the *more* experienced or better-resourced founder, out of generosity or a desire to signal partnership.

Generosity is a fine instinct and a poor allocation method. If the contributions are visibly unequal, an equal split does not resolve the tension. It postpones it, with interest, to a moment when the company is worth fighting over.

---

## Start With What Each Founder Actually Brings

Before any framework, inventory the inputs without flinching:

- **Cash** contributed, and cash *forgone* (the founder working full-time without market salary is contributing that salary).
- **Work product brought in**: the codebase, the curriculum, the client methodology, the brand.
- **Time commitment**: full-time versus nights-and-weekends is the single most common asymmetry, and the most commonly papered over.
- **Distribution**: the relationships and reputation that will produce the first twenty customers.
- **Operating burden**: who actually runs the company day to day.
- **Replaceability**: how expensive would it be to hire this founder's contribution at market rates?

One weighting principle: equity mostly pays for the future, not the past. A big head start matters, but four years of full-time execution usually matters more.

## "Equal" Isn't Even New York's Default

A detail that surprises founders: if you form a New York LLC and never address the split, the statute does not assume 50/50. Absent an operating agreement provision, profits, losses, and distributions are allocated **in proportion to the value of each member's contributions** as reflected in the company's records (LLC Law §§ 503, 504), and voting power follows profit shares (§ 402). Two founders who shook hands on "equal partners" while one contributed $40,000 and the other $5,000 may find the default rules already disagree with their handshake. (General partnerships flip the other way: equal shares regardless of contribution.) Either way, the lesson is the same: if you do not write the split down, New York writes it for you, and you may not like its answer.

## Three Ways to Get to a Number

**1. Contribution-weighted scoring.** List the categories above, weight them for your business (distribution might be worth double in a sales-led company), score each founder candidly, and translate to percentages. The output is not scientific and does not need to be. The value is the argument it forces: every gap between self-perception and the scorecard is a conversation you needed to have anyway.

**2. A dynamic split.** Instead of fixing percentages on day one, ownership accrues based on logged contributions (hours, cash, deliverables) until a defined freeze event such as a financing or a revenue threshold. This approach, popularized as "Slicing Pie," is the most honest when the future is murky, and the most administratively demanding, since it only works if the logging actually happens. Best for founder pairs with real uncertainty about relative commitment.

**3. Equal control, unequal economics.** The most useful and least known option, and a place where the LLC form earns its keep. An operating agreement can separate the three things a percentage usually bundles: votes, profits, and capital. You can hold governance at 50/50 (equal partners in every decision) while the economics reflect reality: the cash founder's contribution comes back first through a distribution preference or is treated as a loan; the full-time founder draws a guaranteed payment (the LLC version of a salary) before profits split; residual profits can split on any ratio you choose, and can even shift over time. Most "we must be 50/50" standoffs are actually about respect and control, not money. Give control its own dial and the money conversation gets much easier.

## Let Time Referee: Vesting as the Fallback

When founders cannot agree whether the split should be 50/50 or 60/40, the best answer is often: grant equal, and let both interests vest. A founder who leaves in month ten self-corrects the split through the vesting schedule, and the founder who stays four years earns the equality in fact. Vesting converts an unresolvable argument about the future into a mechanical outcome of the future. ## Anti-Patterns Worth Naming

A few equity moves that reliably age badly: granting real points for a name on an advisory slide; paying for one-time early help (a logo, an intro, a build sprint) with permanent equity instead of cash or a contractor agreement; and the deferral, "we'll fix the split later when things are clearer." Later never has lower stakes. Repricing ownership after the company has value is the hardest negotiation in small-company life, and it can carry tax consequences that a day-one allocation never would.

---

## Conclusion

There is nothing wrong with 50/50 when it reflects reality; two founders, both full-time, comparable contributions, is a real and common pattern. The failure mode is 50/50 as conflict avoidance. Run the inventory, pick a framework, and remember that the LLC gives you more dials than a single percentage: control, cash-out priority, draws, and vesting can each carry part of the load. The goal is a split both founders can still defend out loud in year three.
